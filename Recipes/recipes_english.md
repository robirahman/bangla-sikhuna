\# Bengali Classics Cookbook



\## Aloo Posto (আলু পোস্তো) – Potatoes in Poppy Seeds (Vegetarian, West Bengal)



Aloo Posto is a classic Bengali vegetarian dish from West Bengal. It features tender potato cubes cooked in a mild paste of white poppy seeds, typically tempered in mustard oil:contentReference\[oaicite:0]{index=0}:contentReference\[oaicite:1]{index=1}. With minimal spices, this simple recipe relies on technique and ingredients to make a comforting everyday meal:contentReference\[oaicite:2]{index=2}.



\### Ingredients

\- 500 g potatoes (peeled and diced)  

\- 25 g white poppy seeds (posto dana)  

\- 1 teaspoon nigella seeds (kalonji)  

\- 2–3 green chilies (slit)  

\- 1 red chili (whole)  

\- 2 tablespoons mustard oil  

\- 5 ml (1 tsp) turmeric powder  

\- 5 g salt (adjust to taste)  

\- 100 ml water (as needed)  



\### Instructions

1\. Grind the poppy seeds with 1 green chili into a smooth paste, adding a little water if needed.  

2\. Heat mustard oil in a pan. Add nigella seeds and the whole red chili; stir until fragrant.  

3\. Add the diced potatoes, turmeric, and salt. Stir well to coat the potatoes with oil and spices.  

4\. Add the poppy-seed paste and 100 ml water. Mix and cover the pan. Cook on low heat until the potatoes are tender.  

5\. Check seasoning, then remove from heat. Serve hot with rice or flatbreads.



\## Shukto (শুকতো) – Mixed Vegetable Curry (Vegetarian, West Bengal)



Shukto is a traditional Bengali mixed-vegetable curry, often served as the first course of a meal:contentReference\[oaicite:3]{index=3}. It combines assorted vegetables (such as bitter gourd, eggplant, plantain, potato, and sweet potato) cooked in a lightly spiced sauce. The curry is flavored with mustard oil and panch phoron (Bengali five-spice mix):contentReference\[oaicite:4]{index=4}:contentReference\[oaicite:5]{index=5}, giving it a unique savory-and-bitter profile typical of Bengali cuisine.



\### Ingredients

\- Mixed vegetables (each ~100 g): bitter gourd, eggplant, potato, sweet potato, plantain, green beans, etc.  

\- 2 tablespoons mustard oil  

\- 1 bay leaf  

\- 1 teaspoon whole cumin seeds  

\- 1 teaspoon fennel seeds  

\- ½ teaspoon nigella seeds  

\- ½ teaspoon fenugreek seeds \*(panch phoron spices)\*  

\- ½ cup grated coconut  

\- 1 tablespoon poppy seeds  

\- 1 tablespoon mustard seeds  

\- 1 teaspoon turmeric powder  

\- 1 teaspoon sugar (or jaggery)  

\- 5 g salt (to taste)  

\- 300 ml water  



\### Instructions

1\. Wash and cut all vegetables into similar-sized pieces.  

2\. Grind coconut, poppy seeds, and mustard seeds with a little water into a smooth paste.  

3\. Heat mustard oil in a pan. Add the bay leaf and all the whole panch phoron spices; sauté briefly.  

4\. Add the vegetables (except plantain) and stir for a few minutes.  

5\. Sprinkle turmeric, salt, and sugar; mix. Add the ground paste and water. Stir well.  

6\. Cover and simmer on low heat until the vegetables are fully cooked and the flavors meld.  

7\. Finally, add the plantain, cook a few more minutes, then remove from heat. Serve hot with rice or puris.



\## Kosha Mangsho (কষা মাংস) – Slow-Cooked Mutton Curry (Non-Vegetarian, West Bengal)



Kosha Mangsho is a beloved Bengali mutton curry, known for its rich, dark gravy and tender meat. The term "kosha" refers to the slow-braising technique (similar to "bhuna") that caramelizes onions and spices:contentReference\[oaicite:6]{index=6}. Goat meat (mutton) is marinated and then cooked very slowly until it is melt-in-your-mouth tender:contentReference\[oaicite:7]{index=7}, making this a festive dish often prepared for celebrations and family gatherings.



\### Ingredients

\- 500 g goat meat (mutton), cut into pieces  

\- 2 tablespoons mustard oil  

\- 2 large onions (finely sliced or pureed)  

\- 1 tablespoon ginger paste  

\- 1 tablespoon garlic paste  

\- 1 green chili (slit)  

\- 100 g plain yogurt  

\- ½ teaspoon turmeric powder  

\- 1 teaspoon cumin powder  

\- 1 teaspoon coriander powder  

\- 1 teaspoon red chili powder  

\- 1 teaspoon garam masala powder  

\- 5 g salt (to taste)  

\- 2 tablespoons ghee (clarified butter) or oil  



\### Instructions

1\. Marinate the meat with yogurt, turmeric, cumin, coriander, red chili powder, and a pinch of salt for 1–2 hours:contentReference\[oaicite:8]{index=8}.  

2\. Heat mustard oil and ghee in a deep pot. Add the sliced onions and sauté slowly until they turn deep brown and caramelize.  

3\. Add ginger-garlic paste and green chili; cook for 2–3 minutes until the raw smell disappears.  

4\. Add the marinated mutton and brown it on all sides.  

5\. Sprinkle salt, then pour in a little water (about 100 ml). Cover and simmer on very low heat. Cook slowly for 1–1.5 hours, stirring occasionally, until the meat is extremely tender and the gravy thickens.  

6\. Stir in garam masala, adjust seasoning, and serve hot. Enjoy with rice, paratha, or luchi.



\## Sandesh (সন্দেশ) – Bengali Cheese Fudge (Sweet, West Bengal)



Sandesh is a popular Bengali sweet from the Bengal region, made with fresh chhena (cottage cheese) and sugar:contentReference\[oaicite:9]{index=9}. It is often flavored with cardamom or saffron and shaped into various decorative forms. Sandesh is a must-have dessert at festivals and special occasions in West Bengal and Bangladesh.



\### Ingredients

\- 250 g fresh chhena (paneer or homemade cottage cheese)  

\- 50 g powdered sugar (adjust to taste)  

\- ¼ teaspoon cardamom powder  

\- A pinch of saffron strands (optional)  

\- 1 teaspoon milk (if needed to bind the dough)  



\### Instructions

1\. Crumble or knead the chhena until it is smooth and free of lumps.  

2\. Place the chhena in a non-stick pan over low heat and gently cook, stirring constantly.  

3\. Once the chhena starts to thicken, add the sugar and continue stirring. The mixture will become dough-like.  

4\. Mix in the cardamom powder (and saffron if using). If the mixture is too dry, add a teaspoon of milk. Cook for a minute more.  

5\. Remove from heat. Divide the warm mixture and shape it into small discs or desired shapes (using a little butter or foil to prevent sticking).  

6\. Let the Sandesh cool and set slightly. Serve at room temperature or chilled.



\## Mishti Doi (মিষ্টি দই) – Sweet Fermented Yogurt (Sweet, Bangladesh)



Mishti Doi is a traditional sweet yogurt from Bengal (especially Bangladesh):contentReference\[oaicite:10]{index=10}:contentReference\[oaicite:11]{index=11}. It is made by fermenting thickened milk sweetened with jaggery (or sugar) and is set in clay pots. The result is a creamy, caramel-flavored dessert that is enjoyed chilled.



\### Ingredients

\- 500 ml whole milk  

\- 50 g palm jaggery (date palm sugar, "nolen gur") or brown sugar  

\- 2 tablespoons plain yogurt (for starter culture)  

\- ¼ teaspoon cardamom powder (optional)  

\- Earthen pot or bowl (traditional setting, optional)  



\### Instructions

1\. Boil the milk and reduce it by about one-third (to ~350 ml), stirring to prevent burning.  

2\. Remove from heat and let the milk cool until warm (around 40–45 °C).  

3\. Dissolve the jaggery (or sugar) into the warm milk, then stir in the cardamom powder if using.  

4\. Mix in the yogurt starter until fully incorporated.  

5\. Pour the mixture into a clean pot or clay bowl. Cover and keep it in a warm place (or in an oven with the light on) for 6–12 hours until set.  

6\. Once set, refrigerate. Serve the Mishti Doi cold as a dessert.



\## Shorshe Ilish (সরষে ইলিশ) – Hilsa Fish in Mustard (Non-Vegetarian, Bangladesh)



Shorshe Ilish is a classic Bengali fish curry from Bangladesh, featuring hilsa (ilish) fish cooked in a mustard sauce. Fresh mustard seeds are ground into a paste and cooked with mustard oil and green chilies to create a sharp, flavorful gravy. This beloved dish showcases the tender hilsa with its oil-rich flesh and is traditionally served with steamed rice.



\### Ingredients

\- 4 pieces hilsa fish (ilish), about 200 g each  

\- 4 tablespoons mustard seeds (ground to a paste)  

\- 1 tablespoon poppy seeds (optional, for creaminess)  

\- 3 tablespoons mustard oil  

\- 2–3 green chilies  

\- ½ teaspoon turmeric powder  

\- 5 g salt (or to taste)  

\- A pinch of sugar  

\- 100 ml water  



\### Instructions

1\. Grind the mustard seeds (and poppy seeds, if using) with 2 green chilies into a fine paste.  

2\. Heat mustard oil in a pan. Add a pinch of salt and the fish pieces; fry gently on both sides until lightly seared. Remove and set aside.  

3\. In the same oil, add the mustard paste. Stir for a minute (do not let it burn).  

4\. Add turmeric, salt, a pinch of sugar, and 100 ml water. Stir and bring to a simmer.  

5\. Carefully return the fried hilsa pieces to the pan. Cover and cook on low heat for 7–10 minutes until the fish is cooked through and the oil separates slightly.  

6\. Garnish with remaining green chilies. Serve hot with rice.



\## Chingri Malai Curry (চিংড়ি মালাই কারি) – Prawn Coconut Curry (Non-Vegetarian, Bengali)



Chingri Malai Curry (Chingri Malaikari) is a festive Bengali prawn curry cooked in creamy coconut milk:contentReference\[oaicite:12]{index=12}. Large prawns are simmered in a mildly spiced coconut-milk sauce, resulting in a rich yet subtle flavor. This elegant dish is popular for special occasions in both West Bengal and Bangladesh.



\### Ingredients

\- 300 g large prawns (cleaned and deveined)  

\- 2 tablespoons mustard oil  

\- 1 large onion (sliced)  

\- 1 tablespoon ginger paste  

\- 1 tablespoon garlic paste  

\- 1 green chili (slit)  

\- 200 ml coconut milk  

\- 100 ml water or stock  

\- ½ teaspoon turmeric powder  

\- 5 g salt (to taste)  

\- 1 teaspoon sugar  

\- Optional: 1 bay leaf, a few whole black peppercorns  



\### Instructions

1\. Heat mustard oil in a pan. Sauté the sliced onion until soft.  

2\. Add ginger-garlic paste and green chili; cook for 1–2 minutes.  

3\. Sprinkle turmeric and salt, then add the prawns. Stir until the prawns start to turn pink.  

4\. Pour in the coconut milk and water/stock. Bring to a gentle simmer.  

5\. Add sugar and simmer for 3–5 minutes until the sauce thickens slightly and the prawns are fully cooked.  

6\. Adjust salt. Serve Chingri Malai Curry hot with rice.



\## Dhokar Dalna (ঢোকা দালনা) – Lentil Cake Curry (Vegetarian, Bangladesh)



Dhokar Dalna is a traditional Bengali vegetarian curry from Bangladesh made with fried lentil cakes in a spiced gravy. It is cooked without onion or garlic (নিরামিষ) and often prepared for special occasions:contentReference\[oaicite:13]{index=13}. The small diamond-shaped cakes (dhoka), made from ground chana dal, are simmered in a rich curry of spices, coconut, and nuts.



\### Ingredients

\- 200 g chana dal (split chickpeas)  

\- 3 green chilies  

\- ½ cup grated coconut  

\- 2 tablespoons mustard oil  

\- ½ teaspoon cumin seeds  

\- 1 teaspoon ginger paste  

\- 1 teaspoon turmeric powder  

\- 5 g salt  

\- 2 bay leaves  

\- 3 dry red chilies  

\- 10 g sugar  

\- 500 ml water (for gravy)  



\### Instructions

1\. Soak chana dal for 2 hours. Grind coarsely with 2 green chilies, adding a little water. Squeeze out any extra water.  

2\. Mix the ground dal with a pinch of salt. Shape into small flat diamond-shaped cakes (dhokas).  

3\. Heat oil in a pan and fry the dhokas until golden brown. Drain and set aside.  

4\. In the same oil, add cumin seeds, bay leaves, and dry red chilies; sauté briefly.  

5\. Add ginger paste, turmeric, and a pinch of salt; sauté for another minute. Pour in 1/2 cup water and bring to a simmer.  

6\. Stir in the grated coconut and sugar. Add the fried dhokas and remaining water. Gently mix and cover. Cook for 5–7 minutes until the flavors meld.  

7\. Adjust salt. Serve the curry hot with rice.



\## Panta Bhat (পান্তা ভাত) – Fermented Rice (Vegetarian, Bangladesh)



Panta Bhat is a traditional fermented rice dish popular in Bangladesh and rural Bengal:contentReference\[oaicite:14]{index=14}:contentReference\[oaicite:15]{index=15}. It is simply cooked rice soaked overnight in water, allowing natural fermentation. The resulting sour, chilled rice is refreshing and nutritious. Panta Bhat is typically served with salt, chopped onion, green chili, and fried or mashed sides (such as potatoes or fish).



\### Ingredients

\- 200 g cooked rice (preferably parboiled rice)  

\- Water (enough to cover the rice)  

\- Salt (to taste)  

\- 1-2 green chilies (chopped)  

\- Optional accompaniments: sliced onion, lemon, mashed vegetables, fried fish, or pickles  



\### Instructions

1\. Take leftover cooked rice and rinse briefly. Drain well.  

2\. Place the rice in a clean pot or bowl. Cover it completely with fresh water (about 1.5–2 liters).  

3\. Cover the container and leave it at room temperature for 8–12 hours (overnight) to ferment slightly:contentReference\[oaicite:16]{index=16}:contentReference\[oaicite:17]{index=17}.  

4\. Before eating, drain most of the water. Mix in salt and chopped green chilies.  

5\. Serve the Panta Bhat cold, with sliced onion, lemon, or fried fish on the side. It is a cooling comfort food, especially in summer.



\## Rasgulla (রসগোল্লা) – Spongy Cheese Balls in Syrup (Sweet, Bengali)



Rasgulla (rosogolla) is a famous Bengali sweet dumpling made from fresh chhena (cottage cheese) cooked in light sugar syrup:contentReference\[oaicite:18]{index=18}. The soft white balls absorb the sweet syrup and become light and spongy. Rasgulla is a quintessential dessert served at festivals and celebrations throughout Bengal.



\### Ingredients

\- 250 g fresh chhena (paneer)  

\- 1 liter water  

\- 250 g sugar  

\- ¼ teaspoon cardamom powder  

\- 1 teaspoon rose water (optional)  

\- 2–3 drops lemon juice (to curdle milk)  



\### Instructions

1\. Boil milk and add a few drops of lemon juice to curdle it. Drain the curds and rinse with cold water to remove whey.  

2\. Squeeze out excess water and knead the chhena into a smooth dough.  

3\. Divide the dough into small balls (gulab jamun size) and roll them smooth.  

4\. In a large pot, bring water and sugar to a boil. Carefully add the chhena balls, cover, and cook on medium heat for 8–10 minutes.  

5\. The syrup should be bubbling, and the balls will expand. Remove the lid and add cardamom powder and rose water.  

6\. Turn off the heat and let the rasgullas soak in the syrup until serving. Chill before serving if desired.
